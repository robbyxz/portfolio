PROJECT SNAPSHOT

Business Goal: Develop an automated spam classification system capable of filtering email and SMS messages while minimizing phishing exposure and reducing manual moderation effort.

Dataset: 5,572 labelled SMS messages (Ham and Spam).

Key Result: Compared three machine learning approaches and identified the RNN model as providing the strongest balance between spam detection (Recall) and legitimate message classification (Precision).

Tech Stack: Python, Pandas, NumPy, Scikit-Learn, TensorFlow/Keras, NLTK, Matplotlib.

I. Executive Summary & Business Context

Spam messages expose users to phishing attacks, fraud, and unwanted communications while reducing trust in digital platforms. This project developed an automated machine learning pipeline for classifying incoming email and SMS messages as either Ham or Spam. The primary business objective was to maximize spam detection without incorrectly filtering legitimate customer communications.

II. Exploratory Data Analysis

The initial analytical question was whether the dataset's class imbalance (approximately 87% Ham and 13% Spam) required corrective techniques such as oversampling or undersampling. Rather than introducing additional preprocessing immediately, baseline models were trained on the original dataset to understand how class imbalance affected model performance.

Exploratory analysis also revealed meaningful structural differences between both classes. Most legitimate messages contained fewer than 100 characters, while spam messages frequently clustered around 160 characters. These findings influenced the feature engineering and modelling strategy.

III. Methodology & Technical Approach

Three different classification approaches were evaluated using business-relevant performance metrics: Precision, Recall, F1-score and ROC-AUC. Since the dataset was moderately imbalanced, accuracy alone was not considered sufficient for model comparison.

IV. Model Selection Rationale

Although Bernoulli Naive Bayes achieved the highest ROC-AUC, the RNN was selected as the preferred model because it offered the most balanced trade-off between Precision and Recall. Its ability to learn contextual relationships within text enabled more reliable spam detection while maintaining very few false alarms for legitimate messages.

Each evaluated model remains valuable depending on business priorities:

  • Multinomial Naive Bayes — fast baseline with strong overall performance and minimal computational requirements.
  • Bernoulli Naive Bayes — preferred when avoiding false positives is the highest priority.
  • RNN — recommended when maximizing overall classification performance is the primary objective.

V. Understanding the Metrics

Rather than relying solely on overall accuracy, the models were evaluated using metrics directly aligned with business objectives.

  • Precision measures how trustworthy spam predictions are. High precision minimizes false positives, ensuring legitimate customer communications are delivered successfully.
  • Recall measures how effectively spam messages are detected. High recall reduces user exposure to phishing attempts and fraudulent communications.
  • F1-score balances Precision and Recall, making it particularly useful when evaluating imbalanced classification problems.
  • ROC-AUC measures the model's overall ability to distinguish between spam and legitimate messages across different decision thresholds.

VI. Business Impact

Beyond predictive performance, the project demonstrates how machine learning can improve operational efficiency and customer experience.

  • Risk Reduction: Detects phishing and fraudulent messages before reaching users.
  • Operational Efficiency: Reduces the volume of messages requiring manual moderation.
  • Customer Trust: Minimizes false positives, helping ensure important customer communications are delivered correctly.
  • Scalability: Provides an automated solution that can be integrated into high-volume messaging workflows.
